Her Morning

She wakes up before the world does.
For eleven minutes, it belongs to her.

Then it doesn't.

Sab sambhalna.

Holding everything together.

Dabba. Doodhwala. Nanny instructions. Grocery list. School bag. Husband — maybe. She doesn't wait to find out. She leaves.

· · ·

Or perhaps she wakes alone.

No dabba to pack. No school bag. A choice she made with full clarity, full intention — and has spent six years explaining to people who didn't ask but offered opinions anyway.

She leaves too.

· · ·

Or perhaps she woke at 4 AM. Not by choice.

An appointment at 7. The kind she schedules before the office opens so nobody notices. Her body is in the middle of something enormous. Her calendar shows back-to-back meetings. She has learned to hold both.

She leaves.

· · ·

Or perhaps she just came back.

Three months away. She returns to find her projects redistributed, her team subtly realigned, her chair — same chair, same desk — somehow no longer quite hers. The system sent a message while she was gone. She heard it.

She is back. She has to prove it.

· · ·

They take different roads to the same building.

In the car — one thought, arriving in different words, wearing different faces, but underneath, exactly the same:

Am I building a life — or just surviving one?

None of them answer. There isn't time.

The Five of Her

Different lives.
Different choices.
The same meeting room.

She who chose herself first

Ambitious. Deliberate. Deliberately single.

She watched her peers get married and quietly get sidelined. So she made a different bet — on herself. She shows up fully, gives everything, is often the sharpest person in the room. And still gets told she is "too intense." "Not a culture fit." She softened once. It didn't help. She stopped softening. The system still hasn't made room for her.

"I gave up everything they said was holding other women back. Why am I still not enough?"

She who chose freedom

Consciously child-free. Completely clear.

A considered, unambiguous choice. Hers alone. And yet — she is handed the hardest projects because she "has no family commitments." Her boundaries are tested first. Her availability assumed. The radical self-knowledge that led to her choice is used as a resource by everyone else.

"I chose freedom. Why does it feel like I'm paying the price for everyone else's choices?"

She who is building a family

The invisible load. The second shift.

She loves her family and her work and is made to feel that wanting both is greedy. She is the emotional anchor of her team and the logistics coordinator of her household. Running two full-time jobs and evaluated on one. She was told she could have it all. Nobody mentioned she would have to do it all. Alone.

"I was told I could have it all. Nobody said I'd have to do it all — alone."

She who is trying to become a mother

The appointments nobody knows about.

Fertility treatments, IVF cycles, a hormonal rollercoaster happening in silence under a blazer in a boardroom. The leave she cannot ask for. The grief — sometimes — that she carries with no language for it at work. She performs at peak while her body is in the middle of something enormous. She smiles at 9 AM like nothing is happening.

"How long can I keep pretending that I'm fine?"

She who came back

Post-maternity. Rebuilding. Fiercer than before.

She returned to find her projects redistributed, her footing uncertain, her confidence — that thing she spent a decade building — somehow misplaced between the hospital and the office. She is rebuilding her visibility, her relationships, the version of herself that got tangled up with becoming a mother. She is more unsure than she lets on.

"I came back. Why does it feel like I have to prove I never left?"

Where They Meet

By 9 AM, she — all of them — is already the most emotionally productive person in the building.

Team conflict. Junior in crisis. Peer who needs steadying. Three things handled before the laptop is fully open. None of it will appear in the appraisal.

Phir apna kaam. Then — finally — her actual work.

· · ·

The meeting. She speaks. Precise. Considered. True.

The room moves on.

A male colleague repeats her point twelve minutes later. The room nods.

She doesn't flinch. She has trained herself not to.

But the question that rises is not about him.

If the system keeps erasing the way I lead — why am I still trying to protect it?

She is not just trying to climb. She is trying to change the room itself. To build something that ships great work and treats people like people. That doesn't chew through human beings and call it hustle.

Every single day, she asks:

Is that still possible here? Or am I the only one who still thinks it should be?
· · ·

4 PM. Sixty decisions deep. The clearest version of her left the building an hour ago.

She remembers who she wanted to be. A leader whose values walked into the room before she did. Whose integrity was the strategy — not a liability to manage around.

Kahan gayi woh?
Where did she go?
· · ·

The evenings look different for each of them.

One comes home to a family she loves and a second shift that has already begun. One comes home to a quiet she chose and protects fiercely. One comes home to a body that needs rest she cannot give it. One opens her laptop — still proving. Still rebuilding.

But somewhere in each evening — between the thing she did and the thing she still has to do — she catches her reflection.

Not tired. Beyond tired.

And underneath it — something sharp. Still alive. Ambition. Unfinished. Impatient.

Her body has been keeping score. The sleeplessness. The fog. The hormonal tide nobody at work acknowledges. The nervous system that never gets to rest because rest stopped feeling permitted.

Thak gayi hai woh — par haari nahi.

She is exhausted. But she has not given up.

The system was not built for any of them.
It was built for someone who is not in this room.

Each of them — in her own way, every single day — is trying to lead with full integrity inside a structure that keeps asking her to be less than she is.

She is not broken. She is a woman with a fire that the system keeps trying to bureaucratise.

And she hasn't had one full day — ek bhi din — to return to herself. To sit with the women living the same story. To remember what she is fighting for. To lead from the place inside her that still knows — with absolute clarity — exactly who she is.

The nervous system doesn't lie.
Cortisol is not a leadership strategy.

When the body never truly rests, the ability to think clearly, lead with integrity, and simply hear yourself — quietly erodes.

The Day

8:30 AM

Arrive. Breathe. Land.

Morning meditation in open green space. No agenda. No performance. Just arrival — and the rare permission to simply be.

9:30 AM

Lead From The Inside

Facilitated by Rupparna and Aashay Abhyankar. Personal and moral leadership style analysis. What does she stand for — really? Peer reflection, team dynamics, and frameworks that put her values back at the centre of how she leads.

12:00 PM

Pool yoga. Nadi Parikshan — Ayurvedic pulse reading that sees what she cannot say. Signature oil massage. Steam. Sauna. Treatments that work on the nervous system, not just the muscles.

1:00 PM

Eat Slowly. Actually Taste It.

Vegetarian, gut-friendly lunch. In community. With nowhere to be and no one to take care of but herself.

1:30 PM

Go Deeper

Peer coaching circles. Real challenges, honest conversation. What kind of leader does she want to be — and what is actually standing in the way?

3:30 PM

Soundbath & Integration

Held by Aashay. The day settles into the body. One true promise — not to her team, not to her organisation, not to her family. To herself and the leader she is returning to.

This Women's Day, we are not celebrating her resilience.
We are restoring it.

Ek din. Sirf apne liye.

One day. Just for you.
